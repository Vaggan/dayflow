# Project Philosophy

Status: Draft  
Version: 0.1  
Last updated: 2026-07-05  

---

## Purpose

This document defines the principles that guide all decisions in DayFlow.

It exists to ensure the product remains focused on its core goal:

> Helping families understand their day with calm and clarity.

---

## Core Philosophy

DayFlow is not a system to manage information.

It is a system to **reduce uncertainty**.

The goal is not to add more functionality,
but to remove the need to think about where information lives.

---

## Design Principles

### 1. Calm over complexity

If a feature increases cognitive load, it is rejected or simplified.

---

### 2. Shared first

The family is the primary unit.

Information relevant to everyone is shown before personal details.

---

### 3. Integrate, don't replace

DayFlow does not compete with existing tools.

It connects to them and presents a unified view.

---

### 4. Product before technology

Architecture exists to serve the product vision, not the other way around.

We do not add complexity unless it improves the user experience.

---

### 5. Information over administration

DayFlow should display and organize information,
not become another system that requires maintenance.

---

## Non-goals

DayFlow is NOT:

- a task management system
- a calendar replacement
- a communication platform
- a smart home controller
- a news or content feed
- a productivity optimization tool

---

## Product Debt

We define Product Debt as:

> Features that increase complexity without improving clarity or calm.

Examples include:
- unrelated integrations
- feature creep (news, social, entertainment layers)
- redundant input systems

Product Debt is actively avoided, even if technically easy to implement.

---

## Decision Rule

When evaluating a feature, ask:

> Does this reduce uncertainty for the family?

If the answer is no, the feature is out of scope.

---

## Living Document

This philosophy will evolve over time.

However, changes must be intentional and discussed,
not incidental or driven by implementation pressure.
